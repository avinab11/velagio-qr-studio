# **The Technical Foundation: Raster vs. Vector**

Before we talk strategy, let’s talk structure.

## **Raster (PNG/JPG)**

Raster images are made of pixels arranged in a grid.

When you scale a PNG QR code:

* The software interpolates new pixels.


* Edges become soft.


* Corners blur.


* Contrast weakens.



On small prints? You might get away with it.

On a 30-inch yard sign exposed to sunlight and glare? Scans start failing.

And when scans fail, so do opportunities.





## **Vector (SVG)**

SVG (Scalable Vector Graphics) uses mathematical paths instead of pixels.

Each square in the QR grid is defined by equations—not dots.

That means:

* Infinite scalability


* Perfect edges at any size


* Clean quiet zones


* Precision color control



A vector QR code can scale from:

* 1-inch business cards
   to


* 40-foot property billboards



Without losing clarity.

For real estate—where signage size varies dramatically—SVG isn’t optional.

It’s mandatory.

# **Why SVG Precision Impacts Scan Performance**

Beyond aesthetics, SVG affects functionality.

### **1. Quiet Zone Integrity**

QR codes require a “quiet zone” (white margin) around them.

Raster scaling can distort this margin during printing, especially on:

* Coroplast yard signs


* Textured banners


* Glossy flyers



Vector files maintain exact spacing.





### **2. Color Accuracy**

In tools like:

* Adobe Illustrator


* Adobe InDesign


* Figma



You can:

* Adjust CMYK values precisely


* Match brokerage brand colors


* Ensure high contrast ratios


* Avoid low-contrast scanning issues



You cannot safely do this with flattened PNGs without degrading clarity.
